Lesson 8: From Russian history

"Russian history is a story of resilience, revolution, and cultural depth, shaping a nation of unique identity and profound influence on the world stage."

Reading:

The idea of lasting peace in the world

In March 1985, Soviet leader Konstantin Chernenko died, and Mikhail Gorbachev quickly took his place as General Secretary of the Communist Party. Gorbachev now held supreme power over a vast nation with a large stockpile of nuclear weapons. Meanwhile, the USA and NATO were spending heavily on modern weapons, a policy that threatened economic stability for both sides. Gorbachev recognized that the USSR couldn’t compete economically without major changes. Gorbachev began to introduce openness, known as glasnost, and economic reforms, called perestroika. He allowed criticism of government officials and opened the Soviet Union to the outside world, while also releasing political prisoners like Andrei Sakharov. This newfound freedom inspired people across the Soviet Union, especially in the Baltic republics (Latvia, Estonia, and Lithuania), to push for independence. By 1990, the Soviet Union faced severe economic issues, leading to strikes and public criticism of Gorbachev. In 1991, pressure came from both sides: hardliners wanted a return to strict control, while others, like Boris Yeltsin, demanded a more open economy. In August 1991, a small group of Communist leaders attempted a coup while Gorbachev was on vacation, placing him under house arrest. However, huge public demonstrations forced the coup leaders to step down, and Gorbachev returned to Moscow. However, the momentum for change continued. One by one, Soviet republics declared independence, leading to the formation of the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS) and marking the end of the USSR. Gorbachev resigned from the Communist Party, ending its 74-year rule. Gorbachev’s policies helped thaw the Cold War, opened a path to independence for Eastern European countries, and laid the groundwork for discussions about a "new world order" and lasting peace. Though achieving such peace is difficult, Gorbachev’s actions earned him a significant place in 20th-century history.

Grammar :

Active and Passive Voice

1. What is the Active Voice?

In an active voice sentence, the subject of the sentence performs the action. Active voice follows the structure: Subject + Verb + Object.

Example: The chef prepared the meal.

2. What is the Passive Voice?

In a passive voice sentence, the object of an action becomes the subject, and the action is performed on this subject. Passive voice structure: Subject + Form of "to be" + Past Participle + (by Agent).

Example: The meal was prepared by the chef.

3. When to Use Active vs. Passive Voice?

  • Active Voice: For clarity and conciseness. Example: The scientist discovered the cure.
  • Passive Voice: To emphasize the action or when the performer is unknown. Example: The cure was discovered in 1960.

4. Forming the Passive Voice

To form the passive voice, use to be + past participle. Optionally add by + the agent (performer).

Examples of Active and Passive Voice

Active Voice Passive Voice
She writes a letter. A letter is written by her.
The teacher explained the topic. The topic was explained by the teacher.
They are building a house. A house is being built by them.
Scientists have discovered a new species. A new species has been discovered.

"Books are gateways to endless worlds, where every page turns imagination into reality."

Reading:

I like reading books where....

Television, computer, audio-visual instruments in general, are all gradually taking over what used to be the domain of literature. Here are the opinions expressed by teenagers on these ideas, what reading means to them, and how they feel about the world of literature. Buky, Tel Aviv, Israel "I like reading as long as it's something short. Otherwise, I can't follow what is happening, I don't understand anything, and I got bored. I prefer short stories: you can finish them quickly." Nieves, Badajoz, Spain "I love reading funny books, books that make me laugh. At the moment, I'm reading 'Three men in a boat' by Jerome K. Jerome. And it's really interest-ing! I always look forward to the moment when I can curl up in an armchair and go on with the story. I like it a lot when my mum comes to find out why I'm laughing so much and she can't understand why." Billy, Perth, Australia "I wish someone could tell me why it is that as soon as I've been reading a book for a bit, I fall asleep. I always start off with the best intentions, but after a few pages my eyelids come down and I'm snoozing. When I start reading again, I don't remember where I was, so to pick up the thread, I've got to back a few pages. So I read, and then I snooze again! It means I'm always on the same page!" Jurgen, Gras, Austria "I like reading books where the main character is like me, with problems similar to mine. I want to see how they manage to solve their difficulties. But it's not easy to find writers who know how to handle themes that are both everyday and universal. Only the real greats can do that!" Dwith, New Orleans, USA "One thing I really can't stand reading are descriptions: they're always so long and boring! I get the idea that the writer just put them in to show how smart he is and how much he knows about life. I prefer action scenes and a lot of dialogue. I usually just skip the descriptive bits and go on where the story starts again." Marriane, Auxerre, France "I've got to go to school every day as well as doing my homework; in the afternoons, I do some sport, and on Saturdays I have swimming competitions. In the evening I'm tired, and I don't feel like reading. So, when I want to relax and enjoy myself, I prefer watching a film on TV, or going to the cinema with some friends." Pearl, Taipei, Taiwan "Reading is like a great adventure for me. Starting a book is always a great thrill, and I want the writer to carry me off to new, unknown places, places I've never been to before. That's why I love the descriptive passages, because, if they're well done, they can make me feel that I'm in a particular land-scape, make me feel hot or cold, make me see the people in the story with such detail that I feel I could reach out and touch them."

Grammar:

Present and Past Habits: "Used to"

The phrase "used to" is used to describe habits or situations that were true in the past but are no longer true in the present. It refers to actions or states that were regular or customary in the past, but have changed over time.

1. "Used to" for Past Habits (and States)

"Used to" is primarily used to describe past habits, activities, or states that no longer occur in the present.

Structure:
  • Affirmative: Subject + used to + base verb (infinitive)
  • Example: I used to go to the park every Saturday. (In the past, I went regularly, but I don't anymore.)
  • Negative: Subject + didn't + use to + base verb
  • Example: She didn't use to like vegetables. (In the past, she didn’t like vegetables, but maybe she does now.)
  • Question: Did + subject + use to + base verb?
  • Example: Did you use to play tennis when you were younger? (Asking if something was a regular activity in the past.)

2. "Used to" vs. "Be Used to"

"Used to" refers to past habits and states.
"Be used to" refers to being familiar with something in the present.

Examples:
  • I used to live in Paris. (In the past, I lived in Paris.)
  • I am used to living in Paris now. (I’m familiar with the experience of living there.)

3. Examples of "Used to" in Sentences:

Affirmative Sentences:
  • I used to read a lot of books when I was younger.
  • He used to be a singer, but now he works as a teacher.
Negative Sentences:
  • She didn't use to like coffee, but now she drinks it every morning.
  • They didn't use to go on vacation every year.
Questions:
  • Did you use to play video games when you were a child?
  • Did he use to live in New York?

4. "Used to" for Past States

"Used to" can also describe past states, not just actions.

Example: I used to feel nervous before exams, but now I’m more confident.

5. Differences Between "Used to" and Present Tense

"Used to" expresses something that was habitual in the past but is no longer true.
Present simple expresses something that happens regularly now.

Examples:
  • I used to play tennis every weekend. (Past habit)
  • I play tennis every weekend now. (Current habit)

6. Common Mistakes:

Incorrect: I use to like pizza when I was a child.
Correct: I used to like pizza when I was a child.
Incorrect: She didn’t use to play the piano.
Correct: She didn't use to play the piano.

Summary:

"Used to" refers to past habits or states that are no longer true.
Negative form: didn't use to.
Questions: Did you use to + verb.
Present habits are described with the present simple tense, while past habits are described with "used to".

Writing: The World of Books

Task 1: Book Review

Objective: Write a review of a book you’ve read recently.

Instructions:

  • Write a review of a book you’ve read recently. Mention the title, author, and a brief summary of the plot.
  • Explain what you liked or disliked about the book and why.
  • Discuss the main themes or message of the book.
  • Would you recommend the book to others? Why or why not?

Length: 150-200 words

Task 2: The Power of Reading

Objective: Write an essay discussing the role of reading in your life.

Instructions:

  • Write an essay discussing the role of reading in your life and its importance in today’s world.
  • Explain how reading benefits you and compare it to other forms of entertainment.
  • Give examples of books that have influenced you or changed your perspective.

Length: 150-200 words

Speaking:

Speaking Exercise: The World of Books

Objective: Express your opinion on the value of books while practicing your speaking skills.

Instructions:

  1. Preparation (5 minutes): Think about your views on books. Consider the following questions:
    • What type of books do you enjoy reading and why?
    • Do you think reading books is important in the digital age? Why or why not?
    • Can books influence people's thinking and actions? How?
  2. Recording (2-3 minutes): Use the recording feature below to capture your speech on the prompt:

    "What role do books play in your life, and why do you think books are still important in the modern world?"

  3. Content Guidelines:
    • State your opinion clearly.
    • Discuss why books are valuable to you personally.
    • Explain how books impact society and personal development.
    • Offer your thoughts on whether books will continue to be important in the future.
  4. Reflection (Optional): After recording, listen to your speech and reflect on the clarity and structure of your response.

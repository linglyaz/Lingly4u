Lesson 11: The debate over homework

The debate over homework centers on whether it enhances student learning and responsibility or burdens them with unnecessary stress and limits their free time.

Reading:

The debate over homework

When I get home from school, what do I do? Instead of pursuing personal interests, like playing computer games or designing program or models, I often have to do a mountain of homework given to us by our teachers to fill our outside-school hours. Teachers and parents could spend years arguing over whether doing homework develops a pupil's ability to work without assistance, or helps us complete our education and prepare for a post-high school education more successfully. I believe that for many subjects homework is totally unnecessary, and find that it doesn't do me any good. I think, it's really stupid to copy from a textbook because someone else has already taken the time and effort to write this book and it will remain with me until I leave the school year. So what is the point of duplicating it? It's reinventing the wheel! While busy copying things, it's not easy to take in what you are taking down. However, I believe, homework is essential in learning languages. New things are learned at school, and then they have to be practised in writing. I agree that some phrases have to be learned by heart. From these homework exercises, the teacher can observe what has been learned by the pupils and in what areas improvement is needed. If we could get rid of unnecessary homework, we would have more time to take up hobbies and interests which would make us more enthusiastic and interesting.

Grammar :

Phrasal Verbs: Full Explanation

What are Phrasal Verbs?

A phrasal verb is a combination of a verb and one or two particles (a preposition or an adverb) that creates a new meaning different from the original verb. The meaning of the phrasal verb is often not directly related to the meanings of the individual words, making them tricky for learners of English.

For example:

  • Look + up = To search for information (in a dictionary, on the internet, etc.)
    *I will look up the word in the dictionary.*
  • Give + up = To stop trying or to quit something
    *He gave up smoking last year.*

Types of Phrasal Verbs

  • Intransitive Phrasal Verbs: These do not need an object. They stand alone in a sentence.
  • Transitive Phrasal Verbs: These need a direct object to complete their meaning.
  • Phrasal Verbs with Separable and Inseparable Parts: Some phrasal verbs can be separated, and others cannot.
  • Phrasal Verbs with Multiple Meanings: Some phrasal verbs have more than one meaning depending on the context.

Common Phrasal Verbs

  • Bring up: To raise a topic or mention something
    *She brought up an important point in the meeting.*
  • Call off: To cancel something
    *They called off the match due to bad weather.*
  • Check out: To examine or inspect something
    *I need to check out that new movie this weekend.*
  • Come across: To find something by chance
    *I came across a really interesting article yesterday.*
  • Look after: To take care of something or someone
    *She looks after her younger brother when their parents are away.*
  • Set up: To establish or arrange something
    *They set up a new office in the city.*

Why are Phrasal Verbs Important?

  • Common in Everyday English: Phrasal verbs are used frequently in both spoken and written English. Understanding them is essential for communicating effectively.
  • Natural and Idiomatic: Using phrasal verbs correctly makes your speech and writing sound more natural and fluent.
  • Understanding Idiomatic Expressions: Phrasal verbs often appear in idiomatic expressions, which can be confusing if you focus only on the individual meanings of the words.
  • Important for Proficiency Exams: Many English language proficiency tests (like IELTS, TOEFL, etc.) feature phrasal verbs, and knowing how to use them correctly will help improve your scores.

Tips for Learning Phrasal Verbs

  • Group Them by Meaning: Instead of memorizing them individually, try to group phrasal verbs by their meaning or function.
  • Learn Common Phrasal Verbs First: Start with the most common phrasal verbs used in everyday speech, such as *get up*, *give up*, *take off*, etc.
  • Practice in Context: Try to use phrasal verbs in sentences or short stories. The more you practice, the easier they will become to understand and use naturally.
  • Use Flashcards: Create flashcards with the phrasal verb on one side and its meaning and an example sentence on the other.

Conclusion

Phrasal verbs are a vital part of the English language, particularly in informal conversation. They can change the meaning of the original verb entirely, and understanding them is key to mastering English. By practicing them regularly and using them in real-life situations, you will become more confident in your speaking and writing abilities.

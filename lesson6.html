Lesson 6: Is nuclear power a good choice?

This lesson highlights the Earth as our vital home, emphasizing the importance of its diverse ecosystems, natural resources, and our responsibility to protect and preserve the environment for future generations.

Reading:

Is nuclear power a good choice?

Nuclear power provides us with electricity. It uses the energy stored in the nucleus in the centre of atoms. In some very heavy atoms the nucleus can be split into two smaller parts. This process of nuclear fission releases an enormous amount of heat, which is used in a nuclear power engineering. The process of nuclear fission is very dangerous. So much energy is produced that there can be an ex-plosion, and this is what happens in an atom bomb. In a nuclear power station, fission is controlled so that energy is produced without explosions. The uranium fuel forms the core of a nuclear reactor. Special control rods can be raised or lowered into the uranium. These rods, made of cadmium or boron, absorb neu-trons. This slows the reaction. The fuel in a nuclear reactor is very radioactive. It produces a lot of dangerous radiation which is extremely harmful to all living things. Some of the radioactive substances produced by the reactor remain dangerous for thousands of years. Getting rid of this dangerous nuclear waste safely is a serious problem. A nuclear reactor cannot explode like an atom bomb but an accident at a nuclear power station can have disastrous effects over a large area. In 1986 a major accident at Chernobyl nuclear power station in the then USSR released radioactive substances into the atmosphere. Winds carried them across Europe, and rains washed them down to earth. There was an increase in the amount of radiation, grass and crops became radioactive. People living near Chernobyl have suffered very much, and some have died. Nuclear power could provide electricity for hundreds of thousands of years, but is it safe? Should we build more nuclear power stations and increase the chance of terrible accidents? The problem is that, without nuclear power, it will be difficult to provide enough electricity for all future needs. Some people say that we have to accept the risks. Others say that the risks are too great: we should find other ways of generating electricity or adopt a simpler lifestyle that requires less electricity.

Vocabulary:

1. nucleus
ядро
The central and most important part of an object, movement, or group, forming the basis for its activity and growth.
2. to absorb neutrons
нейтрондарды сіңіру
To take in or soak up neutrons, which are subatomic particles found in the nucleus of atoms.
3. to split
бөліну
To divide or break into parts, especially in the context of nuclear reactions.
4. nuclear fission
ядролық бөліну
The process by which a heavy atomic nucleus splits into two smaller nuclei, releasing energy.
5. explosion
жарылыс
A violent and sudden release of energy, often producing a blast or shockwave.
6. uranium fuel
уран отыны
Uranium used as a fuel source in nuclear reactors, often in the form of uranium-235.
7. core of a nuclear reactor
ядерлік реактордың өзегі
The central part of a nuclear reactor where the nuclear reactions take place.
8. control rods
басқару таяқшалары
Rods made of materials that absorb neutrons, used to control the rate of the nuclear reaction in a reactor.
9. radioactive substance
радиоактивті зат
A material that emits radiation as a result of the decay of atomic nuclei.
10. to get rid (of)
құтылу (от)
To remove or dispose of something that is unwanted or unnecessary.
11. waste
қалдықтар
Unwanted or unused materials that are discarded or left over from a process.
12. disastrous effect
апатты әсер
A harmful or catastrophic result, often leading to severe damage or destruction.
13. to suffer
азап шегу
To experience pain, distress, or hardship.
14. to adopt
қабылдау
To choose or take up a practice, idea, or method.

Grammar:

Grammar Explanation: Modal Verbs

Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that express necessity, possibility, ability, or permission. Common modal verbs include:

  • Must: Indicates necessity or strong obligation (e.g., "You must wear a seatbelt.")
  • Might: Indicates possibility (e.g., "It might rain today.")
  • Should: Indicates recommendation or advice (e.g., "You should see a doctor.")
  • Could: Indicates a possibility or ability in the past (e.g., "I could swim when I was younger.")
  • Can: Indicates ability or permission (e.g., "I can drive a car.")

Writing

Task 1: Understanding Nuclear Power

Objective: Write about the advantages and disadvantages of nuclear power as an energy source.

Instructions:

  • Describe how nuclear power works and its role in the global energy mix.
  • Explain the potential benefits of using nuclear energy (e.g., reduced carbon emissions, large energy output).
  • Discuss the risks and concerns associated with nuclear energy (e.g., safety risks, waste disposal, environmental impact).

Length: 100-150 words

Task 2: Evaluating Nuclear Power

Objective: Write an essay evaluating whether nuclear power is a good choice for the future of energy production.

Instructions:

  • Discuss the pros and cons of nuclear power, considering factors like energy efficiency, safety, environmental impact, and long-term sustainability.
  • Explain why some countries are investing in nuclear power, while others are phasing it out.
  • Share your personal opinion on whether nuclear power should be a key part of our energy future and why.

Length: 100-150 words
